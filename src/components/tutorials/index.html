C-SAR

Let's talk about sensors!

Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) is an engineering method used to achieve high-resolution images. The sensors are side-looking, as opposed to down-looking, to more accurately determine the distance of object off which the electromagnetic waves bounce. Like the echo location of bats, the time delay of the echo determines the distance of the object. More on this will be discussed below.

Sensor Type

Active

Sensor emits electromagnetic waves and measures backscatter
Passive

Sensor measures electromagnetic waves reflected by the Earth
There are two types of sensors -- Active and Passive.

C-SAR is an active sensor, which allows it to measure the Earth day and night and penetrate through clouds and vegetation.

Side-looking Radar

orbitProperties_pass

In GEE, we can filter the data based on the orbital pass property. We have two options: "ASCENDING" and "DESCENDING".

Scroll down to learn more
To understand the orbital property pass, we must first discuss the directionality of C-SAR.

C-SAR is characterized as a side-looking radar because it transmits microwaves in only one direction.

Consider the diagrams of side- and down-looking radars. The down-looking radar would recieve backscatter from both the left and right without the ability to distinguish between the directions. The side-looking radar will recieve backscatter from only one direction, allowing us to identify the location of the signal.

Data shadows occur when a large object like a mountain or a tree block electromagnetic waves.

Electromagnetic Waves

Let's talk about waves!

Wave Structure

C-SAR allows us to characterize the Earth's surface using microwaves.

Lets quickly recap electromagnetic waves. Waves have a length from peak to peak, referred to as wavelength (λ), which determines where the wave falls on the electromagnetic spectrum. Microwaves are longer than visible and infrared waves, but shorter than radio waves. Wave height is referred to as amplitude (A).

Wavelength



Wavelength determines the surface from which we gather data. Shorter waves can only measure upper surfaces like vegetation canopies and soil surfaces. The longer the wavelength, the deeper into canopies and soil the wave can penetrate.

Sentinel-1 has a C-band Synthetic Aperature Radar (C-SAR). The C-band indicates that the radar has a wave frequency of 4 to 8 gigahertz. Recall that wavelength is the inverse of frequency. This frequency can penetrate vegetation canopies and soil surfaces, but not as deep as higher-freqency, or longer wavelength, waves.

Incidence Angle


We describe the angle between the transmitted wave and the Earth using its incidence angle.

Examine the diagram depicting the incidence angle


To determine the incidence angle, we first draw a perpendicular line from the point where the transmitted wave hits the earth (orange). Next, we measure the angle between the perpendicular line and the transmitted wave (white). This angle (θ) is our incidence angle.
Incidence Angle
Incidence angles influence our data. Smaller angles provide a top view as the wave is more likely to reflect off vegetation canopies and the ground. Larger angles tend to reflect off tree trunks and therefore provide more of a side view.

While it is important to consider the effect of the incidence angle on our data, we will assume that the incident angle range is too small to have a major effect on the data.

Wave Polarization

Polarization refers to the orientation of the wave. In Google Earth Engine, wave polarization, or "transmitterRecieverPolarization," is designated as V for vertical (depicted in yellow) or H for horizontal (depicted in white).

We can think of the different polarization patterns like the different color bands in optical sensors like Landsat, with different bands building different aspects of the landscape. Each polarization band represents the outgoing, or transmitted, microwaves and those received by the sensor. The magnitude of these transmitted/recieved polarization bands in decibles is used to characterize the Earth.

The HH and VV bands (co-polarization) are frequently used to map water bodies while the HV and VH bands (cross-polarization) are used for regions with rough surfaces, such as a forest.

Data Type

Each pixel in the raw SAR data is a complex value, which is commonly known as power or amplitude. To work with easy to understand values, power values are converted to decibels (dB).

The sensor receives only the electromagnetic waves that it encounters. The higher the decible value for each band, the more backscatter the sensor recieves.

Other Filtering Properties

Swath Measurments

instrumentMode

C-SAR data has four instrument modes, three of which are available on GEE: 'IW' (Interferometric Wide Swath), 'EW' (Extra Wide Swath), and 'SM' (Strip Map).
Each instrument mode has a swath width, or the width of an area on a single satellite pass, and an incident angle.

Resolution

resolution_meters

resolution

Resolution is perhaps the most straight-forward filtering option.
We can choose the spatial resolution of our data in meters squared using 'resolution_meters': 10, 25, or 40.

Alternatively, we can use the 'resolution' filtering option to designate a resolution class for the data. The options are 'H' for high, which is 10 meters squared, and 'M' for medium, which is 40 meters squared.

Visualizing Spatial Resolution



Spatial resolution refers to pixel size. For each pixel in our raster, we have one data point. As the pixel size, or resolution, increases, the detail decreases because we have one value representing a larger area.
